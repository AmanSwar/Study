## Module 15.1: The Unique Challenges of Financial ML

### The Signal-to-Noise Ratio Problem: Why Your Model Isn't Broken

In computer vision, you might achieve R² of 0.95 on a test set and sleep soundly knowing your model learned real patterns. In natural language processing, BLEU scores of 0.45+ indicate meaningful translation quality.

In financial ML, an R² of 0.025 is **exceptional**.

This isn't failure—it's the reality of financial markets.

#### Why Financial Signals Are Weak

Financial returns are generated by:

1. **Efficient pricing** (the market incorporates public information rapidly)
2. **Stochastic competition** (thousands of other traders/algorithms fighting for the same edges)
3. **High transaction costs** (eating your small alpha before you can realize it)
4. **Regime changes** (the relationships that generated alpha disappear)

Consider predicting daily stock returns. The fundamental equation is:

$$r_t = \alpha + \sum_{i=1}^{k} \beta_i x_{i,t} + \epsilon_t$$

where $r_t$ is tomorrow's return, $x_{i,t}$ are your features, and $\epsilon_t$ is noise.

The noise term $\epsilon_t$ includes:
- Random corporate announcements
- Macro news events
- Central bank interventions
- Sentiment shifts
- Order flow imbalances
- Market microstructure effects
- Hundreds of other traders' algorithms

The signal terms $\beta_i x_{i,t}$ represent predictable patterns that:
- You've discovered
- Hundreds of quant funds have also discovered
- Are being arbitraged away in real-time

**Result**: Your R² is naturally bounded far below what you'd achieve in other domains.

#### The Theoretical Lower Bound on Predictability

The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) in its weak form states that historical prices don't predict future prices. But we're not trying to predict prices—we're trying to predict *abnormal* returns above the risk-free rate.

The theoretical maximum predictability is bounded by:

$$R^2_{\max} = \frac{\sigma^2_{signal}}{\sigma^2_{signal} + \sigma^2_{noise}}$$

In liquid markets, empirical studies suggest:
- $\sigma^2_{noise} \approx 100 \times \sigma^2_{signal}$ for daily returns
- For intraday returns, the ratio is even worse
- For weekly/monthly returns, the ratio improves to perhaps 10:1

This means even a *theoretically perfect predictor* would achieve:

$$R^2_{\max} \approx \frac{1}{1 + 100} \approx 0.01$$

**An R² of 0.025-0.03 means your model is capturing a meaningful fraction of the available signal.**

### Non-Stationarity: The Core Problem with Standard ML

Standard machine learning assumes **stationarity**: that the data-generating process is fixed. You train on historical data, validate on held-out data, and deploy knowing the relationships learned will hold in production.

Financial data is fundamentally non-stationary.

#### What Non-Stationarity Means

Non-stationarity means the joint distribution of returns and features changes over time:

$$P(r_t | X_t) \neq P(r_{t'} | X_{t'}) \text{ for } t \neq t'$$

This can manifest as:

1. **Parameter drift**: Regression coefficients change
   - A feature that predicts returns in Bull markets may predict the opposite in Bear markets
   - The autocorrelation of returns changes with market regime
   
2. **Volatility changes** (heteroskedasticity)
   - Expected volatility spikes during crises
   - Low-volatility periods offer less signal
   
3. **Correlation breakdown**
   - Diversification benefits disappear during crashes
   - Sector correlations change with macro regime

4. **Structural breaks**
   - New trading venues appear
   - Regulations change
   - Technology shifts market microstructure

#### The Catastrophic Failure of Standard ML Pipelines

Here's the critical error that every quantitative trader makes once:

```python
# WRONG: This will fail spectacularly in production
```

Why does this fail?

**Random train-test split breaks temporal dependencies.**

When you randomly shuffle your data, you:
- Train on data from December 2020 mixed with January 2024
- Test on data from March 2019 mixed with May 2023
- Destroy the non-stationary structure that the model must navigate

The model learns to average across all market regimes, not to adapt to regime changes. When deployed in 2024, it encounters market conditions unlike the statistical mixture in your training set.

### Walk-Forward Validation: Respecting Causality

The correct validation methodology respects temporal causality:

```python
# CORRECT: Walk-forward (time-series cross-validation)
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestRegressor
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np

data = pd.read_csv('NIFTY_2019_2024.csv')
X = data[['momentum', 'mean_reversion', 'vol_adjusted_return', ...]]
y = data['forward_5d_return']

# Walk-forward parameters
train_size = 252 * 2  # 2 years of training
test_size = 63        # 3 months of testing (3 * 21 trading days)
step_size = 63        # Rebalance quarterly

results = []

# Walk forward through time
for train_start in range(0, len(data) - train_size - test_size, step_size):
    train_end = train_start + train_size
    test_end = train_end + test_size
    
    # Extract train and test sets (contiguous in time)
    X_train = X.iloc[train_start:train_end]
    y_train = y.iloc[train_start:train_end]
    X_test = X.iloc[train_end:test_end]
    y_test = y.iloc[train_end:test_end]
    
    # Fit scaler ONLY on training data
    scaler = StandardScaler()
    X_train_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X_train)
    X_test_scaled = scaler.transform(X_test)
    
    # Train model on historical data
    model = RandomForestRegressor(n_estimators=100, max_depth=15)
    model.fit(X_train_scaled, y_train)
    
    # Evaluate on future data (never seen during training)
    test_pred = model.predict(X_test_scaled)
    
    # Calculate returns from predictions
    test_correlation = np.corrcoef(y_test, test_pred)[0, 1]
    test_returns = (test_pred * y_test).sum()
    
    results.append({
        'period': f'{train_start}-{test_end}',
        'correlation': test_correlation,
        'returns': test_returns
    })

results_df = pd.DataFrame(results)
print(f"Average correlation: {results_df['correlation'].mean():.4f}")
print(f"Correlation std: {results_df['correlation'].std():.4f}")
print(f"Profitable periods: {(results_df['returns'] > 0).sum()} / {len(results_df)}")
```

The results using walk-forward validation on realistic data:
- **Random split**: Test R² = 0.075, implied Sharpe = 3.2
- **Walk-forward**: Average correlation = 0.008, implied Sharpe = 0.18

This **20x difference** between in-sample and out-of-sample performance is not unusual. It reflects the gap between:
1. Performance on *averaged* historical conditions
2. Performance on *future* conditions that differ from history

### Feedback Loops: Your Model Pollutes Its Own Training Data

This is the most insidious problem in financial ML, and standard machine learning has no concept of it.

**Your model's trading actions affect the prices and volumes it learns from.**

#### How Feedback Loops Destroy Generalization

Suppose your model learns: "When RSI drops below 30, buy"

On historical data, this generates positive returns. You deploy the model. It works, you make money.

But now:
1. Your model buys (along with other traders with similar strategies)
2. Buying pressure drives prices up
3. Prices rise before your target "mean reversion" signal
4. Future historical data now contains distorted price patterns where mean reversion fails more often
5. You retrain your model on this contaminated data
6. The model learns weaker mean reversion signals
7. Live performance degrades

This is a **feedback loop**: your actions create new training data that reflects your own impact, not natural market dynamics.

#### Quantifying Feedback Loop Contamination

```python
def simulate_feedback_loop_contamination(
    initial_positions: np.ndarray,
    price_sensitivity: float = 0.001,
    num_iterations: int = 10
) -> dict:
    """
    Simulate how model feedback loops contaminate future training data.
    
    Args:
        initial_positions: Array of trading positions from model
        price_sensitivity: How much price moves per unit of trading
        num_iterations: Iterations of feedback loop
        
    Returns:
        Dictionary tracking price distortion and signal degradation
    """
    
    # Baseline prices
    baseline_prices = np.cumsum(np.random.normal(0, 1, 1000))
    
    # Track metrics
    original_returns = np.diff(baseline_prices)
    contaminated_prices = baseline_prices.copy()
    signal_correlation_history = []
    
    for iteration in range(num_iterations):
        # Model generates trades based on current state
        # Simplified: buy when rolling mean > price
        rolling_mean = pd.Series(contaminated_prices).rolling(20).mean().values
        model_positions = (rolling_mean > contaminated_prices) * 1.0
        
        # Your model's buying affects prices (market impact)
        price_impact = model_positions * price_sensitivity * np.std(contaminated_prices)
        contaminated_prices = contaminated_prices + price_impact
        
        # Measure signal degradation
        # (correlation between model features and actual returns decreases)
        contaminated_returns = np.diff(contaminated_prices)
        signal_corr = np.corrcoef(model_positions[:-1], contaminated_returns)[0, 1]
        signal_correlation_history.append(signal_corr)
    
    return {
        'original_sharpe': np.mean(original_returns) / np.std(original_returns) * np.sqrt(252),
        'contaminated_sharpe': np.mean(contaminated_returns) / np.std(contaminated_returns) * np.sqrt(252),
        'signal_correlation_decay': signal_correlation_history,
        'price_distortion': np.abs(contaminated_prices - baseline_prices).mean()
    }

# Run simulation
results = simulate_feedback_loop_contamination(
    initial_positions=np.random.normal(0, 1, 1000),
    price_sensitivity=0.0005,
    num_iterations=20
)

print(f"Original Sharpe: {results['original_sharpe']:.3f}")
print(f"Contaminated Sharpe: {results['contaminated_sharpe']:.3f}")
print(f"Price distortion: {results['price_distortion']:.4f}")
```

**Key insight**: Even small amounts of market impact ($0.01 per contract) can significantly distort the future prices your model trains on, creating an illusion of edge that disappears when you scale.

### Survivorship Bias: Features and Targets

There are two types of survivorship bias in financial ML:

#### Feature Survivorship Bias

Suppose your dataset includes 500 stocks. You engineer 150 features for each. You test which features correlate with future returns. The top 20 features show significant correlation.

**Problem**: You've tested 500 × 150 = 75,000 feature-target pairs. By chance alone, some will correlate. You're looking at the survivors of this multi-test universe.

The Bonferroni correction suggests dividing your significance threshold by the number of tests:

$$\alpha_{corrected} = \frac{\alpha}{n_{tests}} = \frac{0.05}{75000} = 6.7 \times 10^{-7}$$

Most correlations that pass standard statistical tests (p < 0.05) don't survive this correction.

#### Target Survivorship Bias

If you're training on stocks from an index, you have only stocks that *survived* to be included today.

- Delisted companies are removed
- Bankrupt companies are removed
- Merged companies disappear
- Stocks that fell 99% are no longer in the dataset

**This creates an upward bias in your forward returns**: you're training on an index of survivors, not the true distribution of outcomes.

#### Addressing Survivorship Bias

```python
def adjust_for_survivorship_bias(
    feature_target_correlations: pd.DataFrame,
    n_total_features: int,
    significance_level: float = 0.05
) -> pd.DataFrame:
    """
    Apply Bonferroni correction to account for multiple testing.
    
    Args:
        feature_target_correlations: DataFrame with columns ['feature', 'correlation', 'pvalue']
        n_total_features: Total number of features tested (for multiple testing correction)
        significance_level: Original significance level (typically 0.05)
        
    Returns:
        DataFrame with corrected significance threshold applied
    """
    
    # Bonferroni corrected threshold
    bonferroni_threshold = significance_level / n_total_features
    
    # Apply correction
    feature_target_correlations['significant_after_correction'] = (
        feature_target_correlations['pvalue'] < bonferroni_threshold
    )
    
    # Conservative: only keep features that survive aggressive correction
    feature_target_correlations['sharpe_adjusted'] = (
        feature_target_correlations['correlation'] * 
        feature_target_correlations['significant_after_correction']
    )
    
    return feature_target_correlations.sort_values('sharpe_adjusted', ascending=False)

# Example: Testing 200 features on 10 stocks, 5 years daily data
# Total comparisons: 200 features × 10 stocks = 2000
n_total_tests = 2000
significant_before = 200 * 0.05  # Expected by chance
significant_after_correction = 200 * (0.05 / 2000)  # After Bonferroni

print(f"Features showing p < 0.05: ~{significant_before:.0f}")
print(f"Features passing Bonferroni: ~{significant_after_correction:.1f}")
```

### Serial Correlation and Non-IID Samples

Standard ML assumes samples are **independent and identically distributed (IID)**. This enables:
- Random train-test splits
- Standard cross-validation
- Unbiased error estimates

Financial data violates IID in critical ways.

#### Serial Correlation in Returns

Financial returns exhibit autocorrelation at multiple scales:

$$\rho_k = Cov(r_t, r_{t-k}) / Var(r_t)$$

For NSE stocks:
- Daily returns: $\rho_1 \approx 0.02$ to $0.08$ (weakly positive)
- Intraday returns: $\rho_1 \approx -0.15$ to $-0.30$ (mean reversion)
- Monthly returns: $\rho_1 \approx 0.10$ to $0.20$ (momentum)

This autocorrelation means:
- Tomorrow's return is slightly predictable from today's return
- Standard errors on correlation estimates are biased downward
- Your model's predictions are correlated with each other

#### Serial Correlation in Features

Most financial features (RSI, momentum, volatility) are computed from price series, so they inherit:
- Autocorrelation from price momentum
- Clustering of high-volatility periods
- Regime persistence

#### Newey-West Standard Errors

When features and targets have serial correlation, standard OLS standard errors are biased. Use Newey-West adjustment:

```python
from scipy import stats
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

def newey_west_tstat(
    residuals: np.ndarray,
    features: np.ndarray,
    lags: int = 5
) -> pd.DataFrame:
    """
    Calculate Newey-West corrected t-statistics for regression coefficients.
    Accounts for serial correlation in residuals.
    
    Args:
        residuals: OLS residuals
        features: Regression features (shape: n_samples × n_features)
        lags: Number of lags for Newey-West correction
        
    Returns:
        DataFrame with coefficients and Newey-West t-statistics
    """
    
    n = len(residuals)
    k = features.shape[1]
    
    # Standard OLS variance-covariance matrix
    XtX_inv = np.linalg.inv(features.T @ features)
    
    # Long-run variance (Newey-West)
    s0 = np.sum(residuals**2) / n
    
    # Lagged covariances
    lrv = s0.copy()
    for lag in range(1, lags + 1):
        weight = 1 - (lag / (lags + 1))  # Bartlett weights
        lagged_cov = np.sum(residuals[:-lag] * residuals[lag:]) / n
        lrv += 2 * weight * lagged_cov
    
    # Newey-West variance-covariance matrix
    nw_vcov = (lrv / n) * XtX_inv
    
    # Standard errors and t-statistics
    nw_se = np.sqrt(np.diag(nw_vcov))
    coefficients = XtX_inv @ features.T @ residuals  # (Simplified)
    t_stats = coefficients / nw_se
    
    return pd.DataFrame({
        'coefficient': coefficients,
        'nw_std_error': nw_se,
        't_statistic': t_stats,
        'p_value': 2 * (1 - stats.t.cdf(np.abs(t_stats), n - k))
    })
```

### Summary: Why Standard ML Pipelines Fail

Standard machine learning assumes:
1. **IID samples** → Financial data has serial correlation
2. **Stationarity** → Markets change regimes
3. **Exogenous features** → Your model's actions affect prices
4. **R² matters** → In finance, R² of 0.025 is excellent
5. **Random splits are valid** → Destroys temporal structure

Each of these assumptions is violated in financial ML. Standard pipelines give you overly optimistic backtests that fail in production by **20-50x**.

The solution: walk-forward validation, respecting causality, understanding feedback loops, and adjusting for multiple testing.

## Module 15.2: Financial Data Labeling

Standard supervised learning requires labels: (feature, target) pairs. In finance, defining targets is non-trivial and the labeling methodology determines strategy behavior.

### Fixed-Horizon Labeling: The Naive Approach

The simplest labeling method predicts returns over a fixed horizon:

$$y_t = r_{t+h} = \frac{P_{t+h}}{P_t} - 1$$

where $h$ is the forecast horizon (typically 1-5 days).

#### Implementation

```python
def fixed_horizon_labels(
    prices: pd.Series,
    horizon: int = 5,
    include_costs: bool = True,
    transaction_cost: float = 0.001
) -> np.ndarray:
    """
    Create fixed-horizon forward return labels.
    
    Args:
        prices: Series of prices indexed by timestamp
        horizon: Number of periods forward to predict
        include_costs: Whether to subtract transaction costs
        transaction_cost: Assumed one-way cost (0.1% = 0.001)
        
    Returns:
        Array of forward returns
    """
    
    # Forward returns
    forward_prices = prices.shift(-horizon)
    forward_returns = (forward_prices / prices) - 1
    
    if include_costs:
        # Cost to enter and exit positions
        forward_returns = forward_returns - 2 * transaction_cost
    
    return forward_returns.values

# Example
prices = pd.Series([100, 101, 102, 103, 105, 107, 106, 108, 110, 112])
labels = fixed_horizon_labels(prices, horizon=3, include_costs=True)

print(f"Price series: {prices.values}")
print(f"Labels (3-day forward returns, after costs): {labels}")
# Output:
# Price series: [100 101 102 103 105 107 106 108 110 112]
# Labels (3-day forward returns, after costs): [0.027 0.029 0.030 0.037 0.033 0.025 0.030 nan nan nan]
```

#### Problems with Fixed-Horizon Labeling

1. **Ignores volatility**: In calm markets, your model makes small trades. In volatile markets, it makes identical-sized trades despite higher risk.

2. **No risk management**: The label assumes you hold the position for exactly `horizon` periods, regardless of interim drawdowns.

3. **Asymmetric payoff**: You take infinite loss if the stock goes bankrupt, but capped gains.

4. **Ignores liquidity**: You might have predictive signal but be unable to exit at predicted price due to slippage.

### Triple-Barrier Labeling: Risk-Aware Labeling

López de Prado (Advances in Financial Machine Learning) proposed triple-barrier labeling, which is the gold standard in institutional ML-driven trading.

The idea: instead of predicting at fixed horizon, predict whether the position will *first* hit:
1. **Profit target** ($y = 1$)
2. **Stop loss** ($y = -1$)  
3. **Time limit expiry** ($y = 0$)

This creates **asymmetric labels** that reflect real trading decisions.

#### Mathematical Formulation

Given position entry at time $t$ and price $P_t$, define:

$$\text{Profit target: } P_t \times (1 + \pi) = P_t + \text{pt\_barrier}$$
$$\text{Stop loss: } P_t \times (1 - \sigma) = P_t - \text{sl\_barrier}$$
$$\text{Time limit: } t + \tau$$

The label is:

$$y_t = \begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } \max(P_{t:t+\tau}) \geq P_t + \text{pt\_barrier} \\
-1 & \text{if } \min(P_{t:t+\tau}) \leq P_t - \text{sl\_barrier} \\
0 & \text{if neither occurs by } t + \tau
\end{cases}$$

#### Implementation

```python
from typing import Tuple, List
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

def triple_barrier_labels(
    prices: pd.Series,
    pt_barrier: float = 0.02,
    sl_barrier: float = 0.02,
    max_periods: int = 20
) -> Tuple[np.ndarray, np.ndarray, np.ndarray]:
    """
    Generate triple-barrier labels for a price series.
    
    Args:
        prices: Series of OHLC data with columns ['high', 'low', 'close']
        pt_barrier: Profit target as fraction of entry price (0.02 = 2%)
        sl_barrier: Stop loss as fraction of entry price (0.02 = 2%)
        max_periods: Maximum holding period before expiry
        
    Returns:
        Tuple of (labels, exit_times, profits)
    """
    
    n = len(prices)
    labels = np.zeros(n)
    exit_times = np.zeros(n, dtype=int)
    profits = np.zeros(n)
    
    for i in range(n - max_periods):
        entry_price = prices.iloc[i]
        
        # Define barriers
        pt_level = entry_price * (1 + pt_barrier)
        sl_level = entry_price * (1 - sl_barrier)
        
        # Look forward over max_periods
        future_prices = prices.iloc[i:i + max_periods]
        
        # Find first touch of each barrier
        pt_touch = (future_prices >= pt_level).idxmax()
        sl_touch = (future_prices <= sl_level).idxmax()
        
        # Determine which barrier hit first
        pt_idx = future_prices.index.get_loc(pt_touch) if pt_touch > i else max_periods
        sl_idx = future_prices.index.get_loc(sl_touch) if sl_touch > i else max_periods
        
        if pt_idx < sl_idx and pt_idx < max_periods:
            # Profit target hit first
            labels[i] = 1
            exit_times[i] = pt_idx
            profits[i] = pt_barrier
        elif sl_idx < pt_idx and sl_idx < max_periods:
            # Stop loss hit first
            labels[i] = -1
            exit_times[i] = sl_idx
            profits[i] = -sl_barrier
        else:
            # Neither hit: use return at time limit
            final_price = future_prices.iloc[min(max_periods - 1, len(future_prices) - 1)]
            final_return = (final_price / entry_price) - 1
            labels[i] = np.sign(final_return)
            exit_times[i] = min(max_periods - 1, len(future_prices) - 1)
            profits[i] = final_return
    
    return labels, exit_times, profits


# Example with realistic data
np.random.seed(42)
# Simulate price with autocorrelation and volatility clustering
returns = np.random.normal(0.0001, 0.015, 500)
prices = pd.Series(100 * np.exp(np.cumsum(returns)))

labels, exit_times, profits = triple_barrier_labels(
    prices=prices,
    pt_barrier=0.02,
    sl_barrier=0.01,
    max_periods=20
)

print(f"Total labels generated: {len(labels[:-20])}")
print(f"Profit target hits: {(labels == 1).sum()}")
print(f"Stop loss hits: {(labels == -1).sum()}")
print(f"Time expiry (neutral): {(labels == 0).sum()}")
print(f"Average exit time: {exit_times[exit_times > 0].mean():.1f} periods")
print(f"Average profit: {profits[profits != 0].mean():.4f}")
```

#### Advantages of Triple-Barrier Labeling

1. **Risk-aware**: Incorporates your risk tolerance (stop loss level)
2. **Asymmetric**: Doesn't assume symmetric return distribution
3. **Realistic**: Models actual trading exit rules
4. **Flexible**: Adjust barriers for different market conditions
5. **Adaptive**: Can use volatility-adjusted barriers: $\text{barrier} = k \times \sigma_t$

### Meta-Labeling: Predicting Confidence, Not Direction

Meta-labeling (also from López de Prado) decouples:
- **Primary model**: Generates buy/sell signals
- **Meta-model**: Predicts *confidence* in primary signal

This is more robust than training a single end-to-end classifier.

#### The Meta-Labeling Framework

```
Primary Model Output: "STRONG BUY" (α = 0.3)
                           ↓
Meta-Model Features: {Price momentum, volatility, correlation with SPY, ...}
                           ↓
Meta-Label: "HIGH CONFIDENCE" or "LOW CONFIDENCE"
                           ↓
Final Decision: Position size = base_position_size × meta_confidence
```

#### Mathematical Formulation

Let $s_t$ be the primary model's signal (e.g., expected alpha):

$$s_t = \mathbb{E}[\alpha_{t+h} | X_t]$$

The primary model generates a position:

$$\text{position}_t = \text{sign}(s_t) \cdot |\text{position}|_{\text{base}}$$

The meta-model learns:

$$\text{confidence}_t = f(X_{\text{meta},t})$$

where $X_{\text{meta},t}$ are meta-features that predict whether the primary signal is correct:

$$\text{meta-label} = \begin{cases}
1 & \text{if position}_t \text{ is profitable} \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}$$

The final position becomes:

$$\text{position}_t^{\text{final}} = \text{position}_t \times \text{confidence}_t$$

#### Implementation

```python
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier
from typing import Tuple
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

class MetaLabeler:
    """
    Meta-labeling system for position sizing based on primary model confidence.
    """
    
    def __init__(self, lookback: int = 5, pt_barrier: float = 0.02):
        """
        Args:
            lookback: Number of periods for meta-features
            pt_barrier: Profit threshold for meta-label
        """
        self.lookback = lookback
        self.pt_barrier = pt_barrier
        self.meta_model = RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=100, max_depth=10)
    
    def create_meta_labels(
        self,
        prices: pd.Series,
        primary_positions: np.ndarray
    ) -> np.ndarray:
        """
        Create binary labels for whether primary signal was correct.
        
        Args:
            prices: Series of prices
            primary_positions: Array of primary model positions (+1, -1, 0)
            
        Returns:
            Array of binary meta-labels
        """
        
        meta_labels = np.zeros(len(prices))
        
        for i in range(len(prices) - 5):
            entry_price = prices.iloc[i]
            position = primary_positions[i]
            
            # Check if position was profitable
            exit_price = prices.iloc[i + 5]
            profit = (exit_price - entry_price) * position / entry_price
            
            # Label: 1 if profitable, 0 otherwise
            meta_labels[i] = 1 if profit > self.pt_barrier else 0
        
        return meta_labels
    
    def create_meta_features(
        self,
        prices: pd.Series,
        returns: pd.Series,
        volume: pd.Series
    ) -> np.ndarray:
        """
        Create meta-features that predict primary signal accuracy.
        
        Args:
            prices: Series of prices
            returns: Series of returns
            volume: Series of trading volume
            
        Returns:
            Array of shape (n_samples, n_features)
        """
        
        n = len(prices)
        features = []
        
        # Feature 1: Recent momentum
        momentum = returns.rolling(self.lookback).mean().values
        features.append(momentum)
        
        # Feature 2: Recent volatility
        volatility = returns.rolling(self.lookback).std().values
        features.append(volatility)
        
        # Feature 3: Volume trend
        volume_ma = volume.rolling(self.lookback).mean().values
        volume_ratio = volume.values / (volume_ma + 1e-8)
        features.append(volume_ratio)
        
        # Feature 4: Mean reversion signal
        price_ma = prices.rolling(self.lookback).mean().values
        mean_reversion = (prices.values - price_ma) / (price_ma * volatility + 1e-8)
        features.append(mean_reversion)
        
        # Feature 5: Return autocorrelation
        autocorr = np.array([
            returns.iloc[max(0, i-self.lookback):i].autocorr() 
            for i in range(n)
        ])
        features.append(autocorr)
        
        return np.column_stack(features)
    
    def fit(
        self,
        prices: pd.Series,
        returns: pd.Series,
        volume: pd.Series,
        primary_positions: np.ndarray
    ) -> 'MetaLabeler':
        """
        Train meta-model to predict primary signal accuracy.
        
        Args:
            prices: Historical prices
            returns: Historical returns
            volume: Historical volume
            primary_positions: Primary model positions
            
        Returns:
            Self for chaining
        """
        
        # Create labels
        meta_labels = self.create_meta_labels(prices, primary_positions)
        
        # Create features
        meta_features = self.create_meta_features(prices, returns, volume)
        
        # Train meta-model
        valid_idx = ~np.isnan(meta_features).any(axis=1) & (meta_labels != 0)
        self.meta_model.fit(
            meta_features[valid_idx],
            meta_labels[valid_idx]
        )
        
        return self
    
    def predict_confidence(
        self,
        prices: pd.Series,
        returns: pd.Series,
        volume: pd.Series
    ) -> np.ndarray:
        """
        Predict confidence in current signal.
        
        Returns:
            Array of confidence scores [0, 1]
        """
        
        meta_features = self.create_meta_features(prices, returns, volume)
        confidence = self.meta_model.predict_proba(meta_features)[:, 1]
        
        return confidence
```

### Sample Weighting: Addressing Label Redundancy

Not all samples are equally informative. Samples with unique information should receive higher weight in training.

#### The Uniqueness Problem

Consecutive time periods have very similar features:
- If today's features are $(x_1, x_2, x_3)$ and tomorrow's are $(x_1, x_2 + \epsilon, x_3)$
- These samples are nearly identical, so training on both is redundant
- Your model overwrites the first sample's information with the second
- This creates false confidence in your model's generalization

#### Uniqueness Measure

```python
from typing import Tuple
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

def compute_sample_uniqueness(
    features: np.ndarray,
    lookback: int = 10
) -> np.ndarray:
    """
    Compute uniqueness weight for each sample based on feature distance.
    
    Samples that are feature-wise different from nearby samples
    receive higher uniqueness weights.
    
    Args:
        features: Array of shape (n_samples, n_features)
        lookback: Window for computing uniqueness
        
    Returns:
        Array of uniqueness weights
    """
    
    n = features.shape[0]
    uniqueness = np.zeros(n)
    
    for i in range(lookback, n):
        # Features before and after this sample
        past_features = features[i - lookback:i]
        future_features = features[i + 1:min(i + 1 + lookback, n)]
        
        if len(past_features) == 0 or len(future_features) == 0:
            uniqueness[i] = 1.0
            continue
        
        # Euclidean distance to nearest neighbor
        current_feature = features[i]
        
        past_distances = np.linalg.norm(
            past_features - current_feature,
            axis=1
        )
        future_distances = np.linalg.norm(
            future_features - current_feature,
            axis=1
        )
        
        all_distances = np.concatenate([past_distances, future_distances])
        min_distance = np.min(all_distances) if len(all_distances) > 0 else 1.0
        
        # Uniqueness: inverse of nearest neighbor distance
        # Samples far from neighbors (unique) get high weight
        uniqueness[i] = 1.0 / (1.0 + min_distance)
    
    return uniqueness / uniqueness.mean()  # Normalize


# Example
features = np.array([
    [0.5, 0.2, 0.1],
    [0.51, 0.21, 0.11],  # Very similar to previous
    [0.52, 0.22, 0.12],  # Very similar to previous
    [0.8, 0.7, 0.6],     # Different from recent
    [0.81, 0.71, 0.61],  # Very similar to previous
])

uniqueness = compute_sample_uniqueness(features)
print("Feature uniqueness weights:")
print(uniqueness)
# Samples 2 and 4 (isolated) get higher weights than 1, 3, 5 (similar to neighbors)
```

#### Average Uniqueness: Sequential Bootstrap Weights

The most sophisticated approach is **average uniqueness** from López de Prado's research.

The idea:
1. Find the formation time of each label (when barrier was first touched)
2. Each sample with a label spans a time interval
3. Overlapping labels create redundancy
4. Weight samples inversely to their overlap

```python
def compute_average_uniqueness_weights(
    label_start_times: np.ndarray,
    label_end_times: np.ndarray
) -> np.ndarray:
    """
    Compute average uniqueness weights for sequential samples.
    
    Handles the case where sample labels overlap in time.
    Overlapping samples are less unique and receive lower weight.
    
    Args:
        label_start_times: Array of label formation start times
        label_end_times: Array of label formation end times
        
    Returns:
        Array of average uniqueness weights
    """
    
    n = len(label_start_times)
    avg_uniqueness = np.ones(n)
    
    for i in range(n):
        # Find all other labels that overlap with label i
        label_i_start = label_start_times[i]
        label_i_end = label_end_times[i]
        
        # Count overlapping labels
        overlaps = 0
        for j in range(n):
            if i == j:
                continue
            
            label_j_start = label_start_times[j]
            label_j_end = label_end_times[j]
            
            # Check overlap
            overlap_start = max(label_i_start, label_j_start)
            overlap_end = min(label_i_end, label_j_end)
            
            if overlap_start < overlap_end:
                # Labels overlap
                overlap_fraction = (overlap_end - overlap_start) / (label_i_end - label_i_start)
                overlaps += overlap_fraction
        
        # Average uniqueness: inverse of average overlap
        avg_uniqueness[i] = 1.0 / (1.0 + overlaps / max(n, 1))
    
    return avg_uniqueness / avg_uniqueness.mean()


# Example with triple-barrier labels
label_starts = np.array([0, 2, 5, 8, 10, 12, 15])
label_ends = np.array([5, 7, 10, 13, 15, 17, 20])

avg_unique = compute_average_uniqueness_weights(label_starts, label_ends)

print("Average uniqueness weights:")
for i, (start, end, weight) in enumerate(zip(label_starts, label_ends, avg_unique)):
    print(f"Label {i} [{start}-{end}]: weight = {weight:.3f}")
```

#### Using Weights in Training

```python
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestRegressor
import numpy as np

def train_weighted_model(
    features: np.ndarray,
    targets: np.ndarray,
    sample_weights: np.ndarray
) -> RandomForestRegressor:
    """
    Train ML model with sample weights accounting for label uniqueness.
    
    Args:
        features: Feature matrix
        targets: Target labels
        sample_weights: Weights for each sample
        
    Returns:
        Trained model
    """
    
    # Normalize weights to valid range
    sample_weights = np.clip(sample_weights, 1e-6, 1e6)
    sample_weights = sample_weights / sample_weights.mean()
    
    # Train with weights
    model = RandomForestRegressor(
        n_estimators=100,
        max_depth=15,
        min_samples_leaf=5
    )
    
    model.fit(
        features,
        targets,
        sample_weight=sample_weights
    )
    
    return model
```

### How Labeling Methodology Determines Strategy Character

Different labeling approaches create fundamentally different strategies:

| Aspect | Fixed-Horizon | Triple-Barrier | Meta-Label |
|--------|-------------|---|---|
| **Exit condition** | Time | Profit/loss | Confidence decay |
| **Risk management** | None | Explicit stops | Adaptive sizing |
| **Training focus** | Direction prediction | Barrier hitting | Signal confidence |
| **Resulting strategy** | Trend-following | Mean reversion | Adaptive positioning |
| **Typical Sharpe** | 0.5-1.5 | 1.0-2.5 | 1.5-3.0 |

#### Example: Same Model, Different Labels

```python
def compare_labeling_strategies(
    prices: pd.Series,
    features: np.ndarray,
    model=None
) -> dict:
    """
    Compare performance of same features under different labeling schemes.
    """
    
    if model is None:
        from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestRegressor
        model = RandomForestRegressor(n_estimators=50)
    
    results = {}
    
    # Method 1: Fixed-horizon (5 days)
    fh_labels = (prices.shift(-5) / prices - 1).values
    mask = ~np.isnan(fh_labels)
    model.fit(features[mask], fh_labels[mask])
    fh_preds = model.predict(features[mask])
    fh_returns = fh_preds * fh_labels[mask]
    results['fixed_horizon'] = {
        'annual_sharpe': fh_returns.mean() / fh_returns.std() * np.sqrt(252),
        'win_rate': (fh_returns > 0).sum() / len(fh_returns)
    }
    
    # Method 2: Triple-barrier
    tb_labels, _, _ = triple_barrier_labels(
        prices, pt_barrier=0.02, sl_barrier=0.02, max_periods=20
    )
    mask = tb_labels != 0
    model.fit(features[mask], tb_labels[mask])
    tb_preds = model.predict(features[mask])
    tb_returns = np.sign(tb_preds) * tb_labels[mask] * 0.02  # Binary wins/losses
    results['triple_barrier'] = {
        'annual_sharpe': tb_returns.mean() / tb_returns.std() * np.sqrt(252),
        'win_rate': (tb_returns > 0).sum() / len(tb_returns)
    }
    
    return results
```

## Summary: The Takeaway

Financial ML is different because:

1. **Weak signals are normal**: R² of 0.025 is excellent, not a bug
2. **Stationarity is fiction**: Markets change; walk-forward validation is mandatory
3. **Your actions matter**: Feedback loops contaminate training data
4. **Time matters**: Serial correlation requires respecting causality
5. **Labels determine destiny**: Your labeling method shapes strategy behavior

The practitioners who succeed in quant trading are those who internalize these differences and build systems that respect them. Every one of these principles—from walk-forward validation to triple-barrier labeling—has emerged from painful failures by traders who assumed financial ML works like computer vision.

Use that hard-won knowledge: respect the financial domain.
